Thus, there was due reconciliation of accounts between the Respondent and the Claimant, wherein the Respondent calculated the total outstanding balance of Claimant to be Rs.1698370/-, while as per the Claimant, the same was to be in the figure of Rs.22 Lakhs. It is also clear that the difference in the outstanding balance was largely on account of difference in the sharing rate of coordinators fee. The Tribunal also notes that subsequent to the email of 19.02.2019 sent by the Claimant to the Respondent to have a re-look into the accounts, no further mail was written by the Respondent meaning that it stuck to its position of outstanding balance of Rs.16,98,370/- as on 18.02.2019 even though several mails were written by the Claimant to the Respondent requesting the Respondent to release the payment to him. Therefore, it is wrong to contend on the part of the Respondent that the Claimant did not turn out for the settlement of accounts, nor provided the ledger account maintained by the Claimant, nor supplied any statement of accounts to the Respondent. The email of 18.02.2019 issued by the Respondent to the Claimant categorically mentioned that the Claimant's outstanding balance was Rs.16,98,370/-. The said amount thus seems to have arrived at by accounting for further amounts which as per the email of 04.02.2019 were not considered by the Respondent while giving the outstanding sum of Rs.18,33,287/- payable to the Respondent.
